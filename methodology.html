How We Score

The methodology behind every card on this site

What we do

Every legislator in Washington State files a financial disclosure called an F1. It lists their income, their spouse's income, any businesses they own, and whether anyone in their household is a registered lobbyist. Separately, the state publishes all campaign donations and all lobbyist registrations.

These three databases are public. All on data.wa.gov. None of them are cross-referenced by the state.

We built a tool that connects them and flags structural overlaps between a legislator's financial interests and their legislative authority.

The six factors

The screener checks each legislator against six criteria. Points accumulate. Higher scores mean more structural overlap.

Lobbyist in the household 10 pts

If a PDC-registered lobbyist lives in the same home as a legislator, the lobbyist's clients have a direct financial relationship with the legislator's household. This is the most direct conflict the screener can detect.

Example: Rep. Fitzgibbon's spouse is a registered SEIU lobbyist earning $100K-$200K. They share an address.
Business receives state agency payments 8 pts

If a legislator owns a company that gets paid by state agencies whose budgets the legislature controls, there is a structural incentive to protect that funding.

Example: Sen. Pedersen's employer McKinstry received $31.6M from state agencies. Pedersen chairs the committee that sets those agencies' budgets.
Spouse income over $100K from a non-government source 5 pts

Large household income from a company or industry creates a financial interest in how that entity is regulated. Scored once regardless of how many sources.

Example: Sen. Frame's spouse earns $500K-$750K from Google while Frame sponsors bills regulating online platforms. Google maintains 5 lobbyist firms in Olympia.
Income source matches a lobbyist employer 4 pts each, max 3

If a legislator or their spouse earns money from an organization that also employs registered lobbyists in Olympia, the legislator has a financial relationship with an entity actively trying to influence the legislature.

Example: Rep. Scott's former employer SPAN maintains 5 registered lobbyists. Scott now serves as Vice Chair of the committee SPAN lobbies.
Outside income over $30K 3 pts each, max 4

Outside employment is not inherently a conflict. But each non-government income source increases the surface area for potential overlap with legislative duties. Government income (state agencies, school districts, military, retirement) is excluded.

Five or more business associations 2 pts

A complex financial picture makes it harder for voters to assess where conflicts might exist. This flags legislators with unusually dense financial structures.

Example: Sen. MacEwen manages a venture fund whose holdings are not disclosed on his F1. Without knowing what the fund invests in, voters cannot assess whether his votes affect his portfolio.

What the badges mean

10+ Conflict

Structural overlap exists between finances and legislative authority

5-9 Review

Potential conflicts worth investigating further

2-4 Monitoring

Minor flags, worth watching in future filings

0-1 Clean Pass

No significant conflicts identified in current filing

Two-phase scoring

The automated screener is a first pass. It can only see what the three databases contain. It cannot see city-level contracts, federal filings, or filing discrepancies between state and federal documents.

Legislators with a "Full Analysis" link have been investigated beyond the automated screen. Their scores reflect hand-verified findings from public records, federal LM-2 filings, city contract databases, and other sources the screener cannot access. These curated scores replace the automated score.

What this is not

A high score is not an accusation of corruption. It is a measure of structural overlap. A legislator can have a high score and be acting with complete integrity. The point is that voters deserve to see the overlap and decide for themselves whether it matters.

A clean pass is not an endorsement. It means the automated screen did not find conflicts in the current filing. It does not mean none exist.

This is nonpartisan. We apply the same methodology to every legislator regardless of party. Democrats, Republicans, incumbents, and candidates are all scored identically. We publish clean passes alongside conflict findings because the methodology matters more than the results.

Classification

Incumbent means the legislator appears in the official legislative roster and reported legislative income on their F1.

Candidate means the person filed an F1 for a legislative office but did not report legislative income during the filing period. They are running for a seat, not currently serving.

Washington requires all candidates for state office to file financial disclosures, not just incumbents. Both are included because voters deserve the same transparency from the people seeking power as from the people holding it.
