The Vertically Integrated Campaign

A careless query says Washington's dominant Democratic consulting firm has billed $1.6 million to campaigns its own compliance manager keeps the books for. The careless query is wrong. The honest number is smaller, more recent, and a great deal harder to wave away.

By Sunshine Docket // DRAFT // Companion to "The Outlet, the Office, and the Treasurer"

Editor's note: A data companion to "The Outlet, the Office, and the Treasurer," which sourced the relationships named here: Simpson is a WinPower partner, Pisane its compliance manager, the two are married, and Pisane is the PDC treasurer of record on ninety-nine filings. This piece measures how much of WinPower's billing runs through committees she keeps the books for.

Start with the scary number, because someone will run it.

WinPower Strategies has billed campaigns and committees $13.3 million since the Public Disclosure Commission's expenditure data begins in 2007. That is across 3,950 separate payments.1 Pisane is the treasurer of record on 99 PDC filings.2 Join the two datasets on the committee identifier, ask how much WinPower was paid by any committee Pisane has ever treasured, and the machine hands you $1,625,840. Twelve percent of everything the firm has ever billed, flowing from campaigns whose books are kept by a partner's spouse.3

It's a clean headline. We're not going to use it. The why matters here, because the reason is the whole point.

Three-quarters of that $1.6 million was paid years before Pisane had anything to do with the committee.

Why the easy number lies

The PDC summary table records a committee's treasurer of record. One name, the current one, attached to a committee that may have existed for fifteen years. The expenditure table records every payment, dated. The naive join takes Pisane's name off today's filing and staples it to every check the committee ever wrote to WinPower, back to the beginning. It doesn't ask whether she was the treasurer when the check cleared.

Two campaigns make the problem concrete. They are the two largest payers in that $1.6 million figure, and between them they are three-quarters of it.

Mark Mullet's campaign committee paid WinPower $811,312 across 194 payments. Every one of those payments landed in the 2012, 2016, 2020, or 2024 election cycles. The treasurers of record in those cycles were, in order, Philip Lloyd, Suzanne Naughton, Josie Olsen, and Jay Petterson. Pisane became the treasurer of Mullet's committee in 2025, by which point the WinPower payments had stopped.4 She inherited a paid invoice history, not a billing relationship.

Chris Reykdal's committee paid WinPower $407,196. Those payments run from 2010 through 2024. Pisane's name attaches to Reykdal's filing for the 2028 cycle, a campaign that, as of this writing, has barely begun to spend.5

Mullet plus Reykdal is $1.22 million. Strip out money paid under someone else's treasurership, and the easy number loses three-quarters of its weight before you've looked at anyone else. So we throw it out. A figure that counts a payment as part of the loop when the loop didn't yet exist isn't an upper bound. It's just wrong, and a wrong number on this site is worse than no number at all.

The honest measurement

The defensible question is narrower and answerable: how much was WinPower paid by a committee in the same election cycle that Pisane was its treasurer of record? Match the payment year to the treasurer year, and the loop comes to $174,740, across 32 committees.6 All but $450 of it falls in 2023 or later.7

Which tells you the thing the lifetime denominator hides. This is not an old arrangement. It is a new one, and measuring it against eighteen years of billing is the wrong frame. Measure it against the window in which it actually exists.

Read the table from the bottom up and the dollars stop being the story. Since the start of 2023, 73 distinct committees have paid WinPower. Thirty-one of them, more than four in ten, list Jessica Pisane as their PDC treasurer of record.8 Narrow to the current 2025 and 2026 cycles and it is 17 of 31, a clear majority, and those committees account for more than three-quarters of what WinPower has billed in the window.9

The honest dollar figure is modest. A hundred and seventy-five thousand dollars is not a scandal by volume. But notice what the careful number reveals that the careless one buried: a firm whose own compliance manager has, in the space of three years, become the treasurer of record for a plurality, and lately a majority, of the very campaigns the firm bills. The small dollar total is not evidence the pattern is trivial. It is evidence the pattern is young.

The compliance manager who signs the campaign's PDC reports works for the vendor the reports disclose paying.

What a treasurer is, and why this is the conflict

It helps to be plain about the role. A campaign treasurer is not a clerk. Under Washington law the treasurer is the officer personally responsible for the accuracy of the committee's PDC filings: the C-3 contribution reports, the C-4 expenditure reports, the disclosures that tell the public who paid whom. The treasurer is the person the state holds accountable if a payment to a vendor is misreported or not reported at all.

Now place the people. The treasurer signing off that the campaign's payments to WinPower are accurately disclosed is WinPower's own compliance manager. She is married to a WinPower partner whose income from the firm runs into six figures.10 The party responsible for disclosing the vendor relationship to the public is inside the vendor, and inside the vendor's household.

This isn't an allegation that any report is false. We haven't found a misreported WinPower payment, and we're not claiming one exists. The point is structural and it is the same point the disclosure rules exist to serve: the watcher and the watched should not be the same person. A campaign hiring a consulting firm is ordinary. A campaign hiring the consulting firm and then seating that firm's compliance manager as the public-facing guarantor of its books is the loop closing on itself. Forty-two percent of recent WinPower clients have closed it.

Three chairs, one household

That loop, measured above, is one chair. The companion article documented the other two. Step back from the dollars and look at the shape. Around any campaign there are three jobs meant to sit in three different pairs of hands. The vendor it hires and pays. The treasurer who certifies, to the state and to the public, that what the campaign reports paying that vendor is accurate. And the press that covers the campaign so voters can size it up. Keep the three apart and the design works: the vendor wants to bill, the treasurer is on the hook to disclose, the reporter is paid to be skeptical, and none of them can quietly cover for the others.

Now collapse them into one household. WinPower is the vendor. Jessica Pisane, WinPower's compliance manager and a partner's wife, is the treasurer who certifies the books. And on March 12, 2025, at WinPower's office at 19550 International Boulevard in SeaTac, Pisane founded The Burner, a Seattle news outlet now run by Hannah Krieg that covers the same progressive candidates WinPower works for.12 One household, three chairs. That is what vertical integration is: you own the line end to end, from the money that goes in, to the disclosure that is supposed to police the money, to the coverage that is supposed to tell the voter about both.

Katie Wilson is what it looks like in a single case. Wilson's mayoral campaign paid WinPower $26,814 across 22 payments. Pisane was her treasurer of record. And The Burner ran eight pieces working over Wilson's surveillance-camera policy between December 2025 and April 2026.13

The vendor billed her, the vendor's compliance manager kept her books, and the outlet that compliance manager founded covered her. A reader could see the third chair. The reader could not see the first two, because nobody disclosed them.

The same household that vouches for the accuracy of a campaign's public filings also founded the newsroom covering that campaign. The reader was told about neither.

Be exact about who owes what to whom, because there are two disclosures here and they point at two different audiences. The campaign owes its voters one line on its PDC report: our treasurer works for the consultant we pay. The outlet owes its readers a different line, on its coverage: this newsroom was founded at our subject's consultant's office, by that consultant's compliance manager. The first obligation is the one this article measures. The second is the one the companion piece documented.14 Same household, two silences, two audiences kept in the dark about the same handful of facts.

This is where the count stops being an accounting exercise and starts mattering for the journalism. A single case can always be waved off as a one-off. The 42% answers the question that comes next, which is how often. Not every Burner story about a WinPower client sits inside the conflict. But as a baseline, more than four in ten do: the campaign's books are kept by the outlet's own founder, and the reader, denied the disclosure, cannot tell which stories those are. Wilson is not the rare example the pattern had to be argued from. For WinPower's current clientele, she is close to the median.

The number we would have published

We're leaving the $1.6 million figure in this article on purpose, taken apart but not hidden, because it's the number a hostile or a careless reader will generate on their own, and the responsible thing is to show why it doesn't hold. The honest figures are less dramatic and more durable: $174,740 matched by cycle, and the count that actually carries the weight, 31 of 73 committees since 2023.11

A reader is entitled to ask whether a hundred and seventy-five thousand dollars is worth an article. Fair. The dollars are not the case. The case is that the disclosure architecture Washington built, the architecture meant to let a voter see who is paying a campaign's bills, has a node in it where the vendor and the discloser are one household, and that node now sits under a plurality of one firm's active campaigns. The remedy is not a prosecution. It's two sentences of disclosure, and they almost write themselves. On the campaign's PDC filing: our treasurer is employed by the consulting firm this committee pays. On the outlet's masthead, and on any story that touches a WinPower client: this newsroom was founded by the compliance manager of a consulting firm whose clients we cover. Any reader who's come this far would already write both for them.

Editor's note: This article is a work of opinion journalism, based on facts in the public record. It does not allege quid-pro-quo, falsified disclosure, contractual coordination, or any specific improper transaction between WinPower Strategies, Jessica Pisane, Jacob Simpson, Hannah Krieg, The Burner, or any committee named or counted here. It does not allege that any Burner coverage was bought, coordinated, or written in bad faith. It argues only that the treasurer-of-record relationship between a consulting firm's compliance manager and that firm's own campaign clients, and the editorial relationships documented in the companion report, are disclosable conflicts that went undisclosed, and that the public record supports measuring the pattern carefully rather than carelessly.
