Washington State // SEIU 775

The Closed Loop

How a state payroll tax, an automatic dues stream, and a $13 million ballot campaign add up to one self-funded political machine.

By Sunshine Docket // May 10, 2026 // Reading time: 8 min

Look at your pay stub. Find the line that reads "WA Cares" or "LTC." It's 58 cents on every hundred dollars you earn. No income cap. Deducted from your wages by your employer and remitted to the state. On a $100,000 salary that's $580 a year. On a $200,000 salary it's $1,160. The deduction started July 1, 2023. The benefit, capped at $36,500 over your lifetime and available only after you've paid in for ten years without a five-year break, becomes payable beginning July 1, 2026.1

That's one end of the pipe. This article is about the other end.

What WA Cares actually is

The Long Term Services and Supports Trust Act passed the Washington Legislature in 2019. Prime sponsor: Rep. Laurie Jinkins. Governor Inslee signed it. It established a state-run, payroll-tax-funded long-term care insurance program, the first of its kind in the country.2

The program is overseen by the Long-Term Services and Supports Trust Commission, a body of legislators, agency heads, and stakeholder representatives. Its job: recommend changes, set rules, report annually to the legislature. Fund balance reached roughly $1.3 billion by mid-2024 and keeps growing as employer remittances continue.3

The services WA Cares pays for are largely home care services. Personal care, bathing, dressing, medication management, transfers, meals, daily-living support delivered in the recipient's home. In Washington, the workforce that performs those services is almost entirely represented by one union: SEIU 775. Individual provider home care workers are paid through the state's ProviderOne system, with payroll administered by Consumer Direct Care Network Washington (CDWA), the state-contracted fiscal intermediary.4

The public dollars flow like this. Workers across Washington pay the premium. The state collects it. Caregivers represented by SEIU 775 deliver the services. CDWA processes the payroll. And out of that payroll, the state deducts 3.2 percent of every caregiver's wages and forwards it directly to SEIU 775 as union dues. The dues stream is automatic. Encoded in the collective bargaining agreement. CDWA sends SEIU 775 a "Caregiver Daily Feed File" and a "Deductions Update Response File" every day to keep the dues roster current.5

Who built it

The standard story is that WA Cares was built by a broad coalition of advocates worried about the looming long-term care crisis. Partly true. But the academic record is cleaner about who designed it. A 2023 case study published by the USC Dornsife Equity Research Institute, funded by the Ford Foundation and produced at the request of Caring Across Generations, names SEIU 775 as one of three core constituency organizations in the coalition that built the policy, alongside AARP and Caring Across Generations. The report acknowledges Jess Morales and Madeleine Foutch of SEIU 775 as the people who put the researchers in touch with the key players.6

Madeleine Foutch's name shows up in another place too. She's been a registered SEIU 775 lobbyist in Olympia since 2018. And she sits as a voting member on the LTSS Trust Commission, the body that oversees and recommends changes to the program. September 2022, November 2023, and September 2024 commission minutes record her making and seconding motions on premium rate setting, portability, qualification rules, and program design.7

A union that helped design a state program now has a salaried lobbyist for that union seated as a voting member on the commission that governs the program. Not buried. On the meeting minutes.

What happened when someone tried to make it optional

In 2024, Initiative 2124 went on the Washington ballot. It would have made WA Cares optional rather than mandatory, letting workers opt out of the payroll tax. Sponsors: Brian Heywood's Let's Go Washington committee and state Rep. Jim Walsh.8

A committee called "No On 2124" registered to defeat it. Public Disclosure Commission records show the committee raised $13,329,584. Of that, the following came from SEIU and its affiliates:9

The next-largest contributor outside the SEIU stack: Consumer Direct Care Network Washington, the state-contracted fiscal intermediary that processes payments to home care workers and forwards daily roster files to SEIU 775. They gave $50,000. UFCW Local 3000, the Washington State Democratic Committee, AARP Washington State, and a small handful of other entities accounted for the rest.

I-2124 lost, 55 to 45. The deduction continues.

The loop, stated plainly

Step through it.

The state imposes a payroll tax. The tax funds a program. The program pays home care providers represented by SEIU 775. The workers' wages run through CDWA, which automatically forwards 3.2 percent of those wages to SEIU 775 as dues. SEIU 775 used $50 million in cumulative political spending over the last decade, $17.8 million in 2024 alone, to elect the legislators who passed and sustained the program, and to defeat the initiative that would have made it optional. When the initiative came, SEIU and its allied entities provided 98.5 percent of the opposition funding. The legislator who sponsored the underlying statute, Speaker Laurie Jinkins, has received SEIU contributions in every election cycle since 2014. A salaried SEIU 775 lobbyist holds a voting seat on the commission that oversees the program. The state-contracted vendor that disburses the caregiver payments and forwards the union dues was itself a five-figure contributor to defeating the initiative.10

You don't have to assert anyone acted in bad faith. You don't have to assume coordination or motive. You just have to draw the arrows.

What this is not

WA Cares may turn out to be good policy. Washington's demographics are real, the long-term care affordability crisis is real, and a publicly-funded insurance approach has serious advocates whose arguments deserve a serious hearing. The Milliman actuarial work suggests the fund is solvent under most modeling assumptions. Reasonable people disagree about the program's design, its mandatory structure, and its benefit-to-premium ratio. Those are policy questions.

This article isn't about those questions. It's about a structural question that exists independently of whether WA Cares is wise policy: when the same organization helps draft a law, sits on the commission that oversees it, employs the workforce that delivers the services it funds, captures a fixed percentage of those workers' wages, and uses that revenue to defeat any attempt to make the underlying tax optional, what should we call that arrangement?

The honest answer is that we've got a name for it in every other context. We just haven't been using it for this one.

Where this goes next

The closed loop is one feature of a larger system. Subsequent pieces in this series will look at the architects: the figure who built SEIU 775, term-limited himself out of its presidency, and stayed on as chairman of the trusts, the credentialing pipeline, and the policy nonprofits that surround it; the board interlock that makes the various trust funds, technology platforms, and political organizations function as a single network rather than a federation of independent entities; the recurring pattern of Public Disclosure Commission enforcement actions, each one quietly settled and folded into the cost of doing business.

Each of these stories is its own loop. They all close at roughly the same place.
