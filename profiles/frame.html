Sen. Noel Frame

D-36 | Vice Chair of Finance, Ways & Means | Managing Partner, Uncommon Bridges
Structural Overlap
The core finding: Frame has two structurally independent conflicts. Her consulting firm, Uncommon Bridges, received $1.59 million from the City of Seattle across 22 contracts while she controls Commerce's budget through Ways & Means. Separately, her spouse earns $500K-$750K from Google while she sponsors legislation regulating Google's platforms. No recusals were filed on either matter. Whether current law requires one in either case is a legal question the disclosure system does not clearly resolve.

Conflict 1: The Uncommon Bridges Pipeline

PDC Disclosure · LEB (RCW 42.52.030)

Frame is the Managing Partner of Uncommon Bridges, a consulting firm that contracts with government agencies. Through a public records review, we identified $1.59 million in City of Seattle contracts across 22 engagements involving 6 different city departments.

The timeline shows how the firm's government portfolio grew alongside Frame's political career:

2016 Frame joins BDS Planning (now Uncommon Bridges) as a consultant.
2022 HB 1724 passes 96-1, creating a Permanent Supportive Housing Advisory Committee at Commerce. Frame votes yes.
2022-23 Commerce hires Uncommon Bridges to manage the advisory committee that HB 1724 created. Frame's firm gets the contract for a committee her vote helped establish.
2025 Frame is named Vice Chair of Finance on Ways & Means, giving her direct control over Commerce's operating budget.
Aug 2025 Frame formally named Managing Partner. Firm has grown to 16 employees.

The pipeline: work at consulting firm, get elected, firm builds portfolio of government clients while you serve, move into the committee that controls those clients' budgets, get promoted to Managing Partner. Each step is individually defensible. Together they describe a career in which public service and private income reinforce each other.

Additional confirmed contracts with Commerce include: DEIR assessment, housing policy working group, and condo conversion review. A public records request to Commerce for complete contract values and procurement methods is pending.

Conflict 2: Google and SB 5708

Structural Transparency · LEB Appearance

Frame's spouse earns $500K-$750K per year from Google LLC as a software developer. This is the household's largest income source by far, exceeding Frame's legislative salary and Uncommon Bridges income combined.

Frame sponsors SB 5708 ("Protecting Washington Children Online"), which directly regulates platforms like Google's YouTube. The bill passed the Senate 36-12. Google maintains 5 registered lobbyists in Olympia (2025-2026) to influence legislation affecting its products.

Frame did not file a recusal for the SB 5708 vote. Under RCW 42.52, recusal is required when a legislator has a direct financial interest in the outcome. Whether spousal employment at a regulated company constitutes a "direct" interest is a legal question the statute does not clearly answer. The structural fact remains: the household's largest income source is the company the bill regulates.

Emergency Clause Usage

Legislative Practice

Frame has sponsored 1 bill with an emergency clause during the current session: SB 5814, related to excise taxes. Emergency clauses prevent citizens from challenging legislation through referendum. As a member with both consulting firm contracts and a Google-dependent household, Frame's support for referendum-proof legislation raises questions about whether voters would have the opportunity to challenge bills that affect her financial interests.

About This Analysis

Methodology

Income ranges, not exact figures. All income figures on this page are PDC-reported bands (e.g. "$100K-$200K"), not verified dollar amounts. A legislator at the bottom of a range and one at the top receive the same treatment. Conclusions drawn from these ranges are approximate.

Washington is a citizen legislature. The state constitution envisions part-time legislators who maintain outside careers. Outside income is expected, not inherently problematic. The presence of outside income on this page is not itself a finding. What matters is the structural relationship between that income and the legislator's official authority.

This is an editorial investigation, not a legal finding. The automated screening score on the legislature page is a first-pass filter that measures structural complexity. This deep-dive profile is a hand-investigated editorial analysis based on public records. Neither constitutes a determination that any law has been violated. The Legislative Ethics Board (LEB) and Public Disclosure Commission (PDC) are the bodies with enforcement authority.
