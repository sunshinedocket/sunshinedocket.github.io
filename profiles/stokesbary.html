Rep. Drew Stokesbary

R-31 | House Minority Leader | Stokesbary PLLC | Carbon River Ventures LLC
Possible Conflict
The core finding: Stokesbary is the House Minority Leader and ranking Republican on Appropriations. He earns $200K–$500K from a law firm advising startups and private investment funds whose client list is not publicly disclosed. He owns 100% of Carbon River Ventures LLC, a personal investment vehicle holding venture capital fund LP interests and direct portfolio company equity stakes — also undisclosed. He votes on the tax policy, spending priorities, and securities law that directly affect both his clients and his portfolio. Neither list is public.

The Three-Layer Structure

Stokesbary's financial picture is the most structurally complex in the screener. Three distinct income and investment streams all intersect with his committee work, and the F1 discloses the vehicles without disclosing the underlying holdings.

Layer 1 — Stokesbary PLLC (income $200K–$500K, 2024)

A boutique corporate law firm at 701 Fifth Ave, Suite 4200, Seattle. Per public filings and bar records, the firm advises startups, emerging companies, and private investment funds on corporate governance, securities, M&A, and commercial law. The F1 also shows Chalmers, Adams, Backer & Kaufman LLC pays partnership distributions to Stokesbary PLLC — he maintains an of-counsel role there in political and election law. The client list of Stokesbary PLLC is not disclosed anywhere in the F1. His clients operate in IT, retail, finance, construction, and real estate — all sectors with active legislation before Appropriations and Finance.

Layer 2 — Carbon River Ventures LLC (100% owner, "Family Investment Company")

This is the critical finding. Carbon River Ventures, registered at his law firm's address, holds two categories of assets per the 2024 F1:

  • Venture Capital Fund LP Interests: $100K–$200K — he is a limited partner in one or more VC funds. Which funds are not disclosed.
  • Portfolio Company Securities: $60K–$100K — direct equity stakes in individual companies. Which companies are not disclosed.

In 2022, he disclosed $30K–$60K in investment distributions from Carbon River as income. In 2023 and 2024, the income line is blank — distributions paused or fell below the reporting threshold. The vehicle and its valuations remain on the F1; the contents do not.

Layer 3 — Chalmers, Adams, Backer & Kaufman LLC

A political and election law firm where Stokesbary is a named member. The firm represents Republican candidates, political committees, and redistricting litigants. Per the Chalmers website, he is currently lead counsel in two redistricting cases pending in federal court. The F1 shows Chalmers paying partnership distributions through Stokesbary PLLC. This layer is less a financial conflict than a context item: it explains his deep institutional role in Republican Party infrastructure and his personal financial stake in electoral outcomes.

Why Carbon River Ventures Is the Core Problem

The F1 disclosure system was designed for legislators who own farms, law firms, and rental properties. It was not designed for legislators who manage personal investment vehicles holding LP interests in undisclosed venture funds and direct equity in undisclosed startups.

Stokesbary votes on Washington's capital gains treatment, B&O tax structure, startup securities exemptions, R&D tax credits, and technology procurement — as ranking member of the committees that set all of these policies. His Carbon River Ventures portfolio holds equity in companies whose valuations, exit timelines, and regulatory costs are directly affected by those votes. The F1 tells us the vehicle exists and what it's worth. It does not tell us what it holds.

This is a more acute version of the MacEwen finding (Nova Satus Venture Fund). The differences that make Stokesbary's situation more concerning: Carbon River is entirely self-managed at his own law firm's address, not a third-party fund; his law firm clients are drawn from the same startup and fund universe his portfolio holds equity in; and as Minority Leader, his influence over Finance and Appropriations outcomes is qualitatively different from a committee member's — he negotiates amendments, sets caucus strategy, and decides which bills Republicans will support or block.

The MacEwen Parallel

The MacEwen profile (Nova Satus Venture Fund) identified the same structural problem: a legislator managing an investment vehicle whose portfolio holdings are invisible to the public. Stokesbary's situation is more acute on three dimensions. Carbon River is self-managed at his own law firm's address, not a third-party fund with external oversight. His law firm clients are drawn from the same startup and fund universe his portfolio holds equity in — he may be advising companies he also holds equity in, and voting on legislation affecting both. And as Minority Leader, his influence over committee outcomes is qualitatively different from a backbencher's.

What Would Strengthen This Finding

  • Washington Secretary of State business registry lookup on Carbon River Ventures LLC — formation date, registered agent, any affiliated entities
  • PDC lobbyist registration cross-reference: do any Stokesbary PLLC clients have registered lobbyists in Olympia? Attorney-client privilege shields the client list, but lobbyist employer registrations are public
  • Cross-reference TechNet member companies against Stokesbary's Finance and Appropriations votes on tech-related bills
  • Review Stokesbary's votes on HB 2515 (data center regulation) given TechNet travel in the same disclosure period
  • Review any Washington capital gains legislation votes against Carbon River Ventures distribution timing
